So now we're ready to meet the Korean copula—이다. All Korean verbs end in “다" when in their base(or infinitive) form, but you need to conjugate the verb to put it in a more useable form. We want to conjugate 이다 to 요-form to make it polite (more on the mechanics of conjugation later). When we do this it becomes 이에요 or 예요. Why two variations? Well this normally doesn’t happen in Korean, but it has to do with another fundamental principle present in the language that I like to call consonant-vowel agreement.

Consonant-vowel agreement factors into many languages but is especially prevalent in Korean. It has to do with the awkwardness of a bunch of consonant sounds running into each other. Just imagine if you had to pronounce a sentence with no vowels. Blv m t’s xtrmly dffclt. In English we actually have a fair amount of words where multiple consonants are strung together in a row. Just think of words like “latchstring”, and “catchphrase”. These words have six consonants stuck together with no vowels to break them up! While in Korean these kinds of scenarios are possible, the trend languages take to make pronunciation easier is to have one vowel sound between every one or two consonants.

To facilitate easier pronunciation there are many pairs like 이에요/예요 in Korean. So for words that end in a consonant (meaning they have a 받침) you use 이에요 and for words that end in a vowel (have no 받침) you use 예요. While 예 is technically a vowel in Korean, like the English letter “Y”, it’s sort of a double agent and here is acting as a consonant. Easy enough right?
