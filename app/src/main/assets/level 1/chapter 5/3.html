Let's talk about the words for "yes" and "no" as well.

Word
Meaning

네

yes

아니요

no

저

me (humble)

~씨

Mr./Ms. (postfix)

When asked a yes-or-no question, 네 means "yes" and 아니요 means “no”. We're bringing this up now because 아니요 should not be confused with 아니에요. 아니에요 is used for saying "A does not equal B". 아니요 is used the same way as the English word "no" is to express disagreement. 아니에요 should only ever come at the end of the clause/sentence, whereas 아니요 usually comes at the beginning.

Now we'll look at some examples of how these words our used, but first let us introduce you to our two characters that we will be getting to know in Sero Korean Level 1, 박재영 (Jaeyoung Park) and 애슐리 웨스턴 (Ashley Weston). Be aware that in Korean the family name comes first and the given name comes second. Let's look at some simple phone-dialogue between our two characters.

재영씨예요?

네, 저예요.

(tap for translation)

씨 is a postfix that attaches onto the end of names. In English it is rare for us to use the titles Mr./Ms. with first names, but this is perfectly normal in Korean. Furthermore the title 씨 can also be attached to full names (박재영씨) or just family names (박씨). When used together with a full name, 씨 sounds a little bit intimidating, as if you are calling someone out. When attached to family names, 씨 is often used to refer to people in a general sense, as in "all people with the name Park". Hence, in business or other professional environments you should normally refer to people as their given name + 씨. Let's look at one more example sentence before we wrap this chapter up.

네, 저예요.
