Unlike English family names, Korean names did not develop as an identifier based on people’s occupations. Long before family names became standard in England, Koreans were using native Korean family names. Then along came that dastardly Chinese language and it started to mess things up. Eventually a Chinese character was adopted to write every family name, and during this time pronunciation for names changed too. Nowadays, there are even different characters used to write the same last name in some cases, depending on your branch of the family. For example, while all 김(Kim) family members write their last name with 金, there are three different characters(李, 異, 伊) used to write 이(Lee) depending on what branch of the family you are from.

Another trend common in Asia is putting the “last name” first (to avoid confusion let’s call it the “family” name). Korean names always do this and they are usually followed by two syllable “first names” (which are of course second-names in Korea… sigh). Why don’t we look at some examples.
