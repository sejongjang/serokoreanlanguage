So about K-pop... it's getting crazy popular! And not just popular in Asia, but popular across the world. Although Korean culture has been making strides in Asia for decades a worldwide breakthrough came with the YouTube hit “Gangnam Style”. Psy’s masterpiece of humor held the YouTube view-number crown for years, and besides Gangnam style there’s now at least 35 other Korean Pop (often abbreviated to K-pop) videos with more than 100 million views!

Many people attribute this surge in worldwide popularity to the Korean penchant for “manufactured” stars. Entertainment companies often recruit young teenagers or even children through auditions and scouting, and then they train them for years before their big debut. The companies pair the trainees with professional vocal, dance, and even acting instructors. Those who perform well are promised a future of fame and fortune, while those who can’t keep up or handle the pressure of such a competitive system are let go and return to regular student life.

The critics of such a system say that it’s too artificial and doesn’t allow for artists outside the mainstream to succeed. They also worry about the ethical issues of long-term contracts with onerous terms being signed by teenagers. Furthermore, they argue that company comes before art, stifling creativity.

On the other hand, it’s hard to argue with the success of the star-factory that is the Korean entertainment industry. Advocates say that this system allows people to develop skills far beyond what they would have been able to without the professional help the companies give them. As in all other forms of business, competition drives the bar ever-higher, and the music, choreography, and film produced is definitely impressive. Celebrities are also held to a high bar of behavior and there is great pressure (and perhaps contractual obligations) pushing them to be good role models for the general public. This contrasts starkly with the behavior of other stars internationally who are basically self-managed.

More recently there’s also been a push to broaden the international appeal of Korean entertainment, as evidenced by the recruitment of stars from other countries in Asia and Koreans raised in other countries throughout the world. Many Korean songs are produced not just in Korean, but also in Chinese, Japanese, and/or English. Korean stars learn many languages and frequently take international tours.
