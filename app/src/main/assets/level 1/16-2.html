Finally, the Korean landscape is very rugged. This means that it’s difficult to have large, cultivated fields. Instead, things that grow naturally or things that don’t require a lot of space to grow, are emphasized. The large population of the Korean peninsula squeezes the already limited arable land, so you can literally see miniature farms all over the place. This nod to ancient Korean society is a sight to be seen amidst towering apartment buildings and the modern bustling streets. In these “farms” are things like carrots, potatoes, sweet potatoes, cabbage, peppers, squash, tomatoes, onions, and garlic.

Although much of the food sold in supermarkets is likely to be imported, Korea still has a thriving street market culture. Go there and you’ll find grandmothers sitting on cardboard on the street corner, selling their fresh-picked, organically-grown garlic for half the price of the grocery store. These street markets are hard to miss and are definitely worth checking out whenever you make it to Korea. You may have to do some haggling to get the desired price, but haggling is another great way to improve your Korean!

Another thing to keep in mind with Korean food is that, historically, wheat and dairy are not really part of the Korean diet (recently cereal is becoming a staple in Korea for breakfast). This is stunning since those are essentially the two most important staples of the western diet. What do they have instead? The answer is rice and tea.
