How did North and South Korea split up?

It’s hard to overstate the magnitude of the tragedy that led to one of the most homogenous and harmonious nations on Earth being torn asunder by civil war and ultimately being split into two different countries. The story begins with Japanese occupation prior to World War II. At that time Korea was under the thumb of the Japanese emperor.

We won't go into depth of the imperialistic past of the Japanese empire, but suffice it to say that Koreans were suffering under great oppression. They were beaten if they spoke their native tongue, forced into serving for the japanese military in various wars including WWII, and were forced to change their korean names to japanese in order to conform with cultural assimilation laws. There are many heroic accounts of resistance to Japanese occupation and have lived on in the hearts of Koreans to this day.

In 1945 after WWII had ended, Nazi Germany, Italy, and Japan, having been defeated by the West, were forced to pay for war reparations and give up all the territory they had gained through conquest. As the United States set up a democratic government while temporarily occupying Japan, Korea was then set free to become an autonomous nation once more. You can imagine the excitement, relief, and overwhelming sense of gratitude the Korean people felt after having been released from their 35 year occupation. With aid from the UN, Korea started to grow industrially and began to re-establish their native language and culture into their society, getting rid of any sign of Japanese occupation from the past 3 decades.

In the midst of Korea’s new liberated republic, tensions between the Soviet Union and United States began to grow as the USSR betrayed the western powers and took over eastern germany and most of eastern europe. Eager to spread the newly revolutionized communist political system, the Soviet Union along with China influenced the north part of the korean peninsula and invaded the south. The United States and United Nations, hoping to avoid an entirely communist eastern asian front, continued to back the south and fought alongside South Korea to push back. The battle went back and forth for a couple years ending in a cease fire at the 38th parallel, a line which still constitutes the current border between the two countries.
