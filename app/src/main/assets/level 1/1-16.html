As for what you learned this chapter, I’m sure you’re beginning to see the patterns and how the letters in Hangul build on each other. If you can’t remember a letter, make your mouth into the shape it prescribes. Remember that Hangul letters actually look like their sound! You may be skeptical, but we promise it works and it will help you remember the letters. Next chapter we’ll be talking about the vowel sounds, which are fairly straightforward.

One other thing you’ll notice is that there are a few consonants from English which are conspicuously missing from Korean. We mentioned that there is no "th" sound in Korean, but if you’ve been keeping score at home you’ll have realized that "F”, “TH” “V”, and “Z” are all nowhere to be found. (Rough approximations of “Q” and “X” can technically be reproduced in Korean. R and L are also debatably present.)

These missing sounds simply don’t exist in Modern Hangul. Anciently Hangul also had the letters ㅸ,ㆄ, ㅹ, ㅱ, ᅎ, ᅔ, ᅏ, ᅐ, ᅕ, ᅑ, ᄼ, ᄽ, ᄾ, ᄿ, ㆁ, ㆆ, ㆅ, and ㅿ which corresponded to various sounds like "Th" and "Z" while some are just ancient variations of ㅈ and ㅅ. However, for simplicity’s sake, over time they narrowed down hangul into the alphabetic set you see today.

Instead you’ve got a new sort of sound in ㄹ and some more fun new sounds we’ll be talking about in the next two chapters. Be careful not to mix in English sounds that aren’t a part of the Korean language. You'll find this especially difficult when it comes to "Konglish", or English vocabulary imported into the Korean language. Words like “fitness” and “theme’ are scrubbed clean of sounds absent in Korean and come out as what can best be described as creative approximation of the original English word.

This presents a challenge because you essentially have to learn a new system of pronunciation for words you already know, and Koreans love to drop some English words into their sentences to add a little bit of... panache to their dialogue (see what I did there).

This no doubt has something to do with Hangul being created long after the spoken-language was already well established. King Sejong actually made a system that was more powerful and flexible than the language itself needed. Pretty impressive.

Review these points below and then pass off the Hangul consonants quiz you can continue on your way to fluency!

  • Hangul is made of blocks of letters, and each "block" should be pronounced as one syllable.
  • In each block, the Hangul letters should be written as if you are trying to fill up an imaginary square.
  • Letters on the bottom of blocks are called “badchim” (받침)
  • The t-stop badchim letter are ㄷ, ㅅ, ㅈ, ㅊ, and ㅌ
  • Compound badchim are ㄺ, ㄳ, ㄻ, ㄽ, ㅄ, ㄶ, ㄵ, ㄼ, ㅀ, ㄾ (ㄿ and ㄽ are rare).
  • Korean sounds tend to be more throaty (with the exception of aspirated consonants).
