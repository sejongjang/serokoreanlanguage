나

ㄴ is our next letter. ㄴ is pronounced like “N” (as in “no”). There’s a couple of things to keep in mind with the ㄴ sound. You’ll first note that this letter looks suspiciously like the ㄱ letter that we just learned, but flipped upside-down. This is no coincidence. We’ll be talking more about similarities between letters, but this has to do with the overarching logic behind the alphabet that King Sejong and his advisors created.

Like it’s cousin, ㄴ also has a split personality. What other sound might this letter make? The answer may surprise you—it’s the “D” sound! This second personality usually comes out when ㄴ is at the beginning of a word. It isn’t the hard and heavy English “D” sound that we sometimes say in words like “dog”. Rather it’s pronounced with a lighter flick of the tongue, as in the English word “date”. However, remember that ㄴ most often acts like a less-heavy English “N” sound.

When we pair ㄴ with the vowel ㅏ, it sounds just like the English “N” that you know and love. 나, pronounced “nah”, means “me”. You’ve only learned three letters so far but you’ve already learned how to write and pronounce a very important word in the Korean language—“me”—Hurray!

Before we move on, I told you earlier that we must have at least one consonant and one vowel to make a syllable. While that’s true, like in English, you can actually have more than two letters. In Korean you can put more consonants on the bottom of a syllable block like this:

These bottom letters are called “badchim” (받침), which literally translates to “support”. In other words, the letters on the bottom support, or hold up, the ones on top. Using this structure, we can combine the letters you have learned into the syllable blocks 간, 각, 난, and 낙, giving us a total of six possible combinations, with 가 and 나 as part of the mix (points for rhyming?). You read these blocks left-to-right and top-to-bottom, similar to reading English.

As shown in the chart above, it is possible to put two consonants on the bottom, forming a double-badchim, but keep in mind that this only works for certain letters. The letters ㄱ and ㄴ can never combine on the bottom of a syllable block. When we learn the letters that are compatible as double-badchim I promise I’ll include them!
