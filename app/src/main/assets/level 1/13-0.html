Verbs Part 1

Chapter Insight

Welcome, welcome. Hopefully, you enjoyed that second chapter about numbers. Please settle down somewhere cozy and quiet so you can focus on one of the most important chapter in all of Sero Korean. Here we will begin to harness the power of Korean verbs. This chapter we’re going to be learning past, present, and future conjugations for the 요-polite ending we’ve already been using.

Once you get into the mechanics of verb conjugation, it can get quite messy in many languages. With Korean, the traditional methods of teaching verb conjugation would definitely stray into that messy territory, but lucky for us there’s a better method. This method is excellent because it teaches the underlying logic behind what happens when verbs are conjugated. Again, this is much moreFragment simple and effective than trying to force your brain to remember what happens with every type of verb and verb-ending.

In Korean there are three basic ways we can use verbs:
1) In base (infinitive) form as in 먹다
2) With a connector (like glue) as in "먹어"
3) With a conjugated ending as in "먹어요"

The base form of Korean verbs is useful in many instances but not nearly as useful as a verb with a connector and/or ending such as the infinitive "to eat" in English. Connectors are especially useful because they are the middle step between our inifinitive and our conjugated final product. However, we can sometimes stop with a connector without fully conjugating the verb in Korean, you'll see what we mean later. Finally, conjugated endings are what almost all languages use for formal and sometimes informal speach. For example, we never say, "I to eat hot dogs". We always conjugate the infinitve "to eat" in order to say, " I eat hot dogs" or "He eats hot dogs". See how important conjugation is?

새로 팀
Glue is not our creation. It was invented by the excellent teachers at the Missionary Training Center (MTC) for the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in Provo, Utah. All credit goes to the original creators. In the MTC, missionaries are trained for about two months before being sent off to share their beliefs in Korea (and many other countries). The teachers in the MTC needed an effective method to get the missionaries conjugating verbs correctly in a very short amount of time so this is the system they came up with. The Glue method is being shared here with permission.

This chapter we will be focusing on the 아/어 connector—glue (yeah the chapter title kind of gave it away). Glue is the most important connecter because it is used for creating tenses, connecting words, and of course for the verb ending—요.
