Chapter 18

Asking Questions

Chapter Insight

So far we’ve primarily been learning about how to make statements and confirm those statements. Things like, “This is a chair”, “Jaeyoung went to the store?”, and “It wasn’t me”. While statements are important, they aren’t as powerful as questions are. Now we will learn how to make real questions using question words. Questions open the world to discovery and will open up your Korean speaking abilities dramatically.

You’ve heard of the five “W's” in English right? The words that we ask questions with—who, what, where, why, and when. Besides these five there is the word “how”. Interestingly Korean has a similar feature to English in regards to question words. Like English has the five W’s, Korean has the 7 어’s:

“어디 Where
언제 When
어떤 What type
어느 Which
어떻게 How
얼마(나) How much
왜. Why
Okay I’ll admit “왜” is a stretch, but we can just have it be an exception.

누구 Who
뭐/무엇 What
무슨 What kind
몇 How many

These together are effectively the big 11 question words. It shouldn’t be hard to memorize them because we’ll be seeing them a lot. Wait, but two of these mean almost the same thing! I know I can hear your protests. The words 어떤 and 무슨 translate to “what type” and “what kind” respectively. Just because the definition is similar doesn’t mean they are interchangable, however. To understand when to use them let’s look at their origins.

어떤 comes from an interesting root word—the interrogative verb 어떻다, meaning “to be how”. Interrogative verbs are not a common linguistic feature, but Korean is a special language. You'll also notice that the word for “how”, “어떻게”is also based on the same verb, 어떻다.

None of the other nine interrogative words here are based off a verb. To understand the difference between 어떤 and 무슨 first recognize that for us to use 어떤, the thing we are asking about has to be “how”—i.e. it must be describable, and it must have some qualities that can be associated with it. That means that 어떤 is typically used when you are asking about one specific noun out of a set of preexisting nouns. That means that at times it's also used similarly to the word 어느. On the other hand 무슨 tends to be a little more abstract.
