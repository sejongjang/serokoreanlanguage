이/가 is an important particle that will allow us to use the existence verbs 있다 and 없다. Remember our little consonant-vowel agreement rule? Well it turns out that grammatical markers (particles) in Korean typically follow this rule. 이/가 marks the subject of the sentence (amazing right). That means we can use this particle to tell us whether we have dogs chasing cats or cats chasing dogs. Very useful.

Let's see what this looks like with our existence verbs.

물이 있어요

(tap for translation)


물이 없어요

(tap for translation)


차가 있어요

(tap for translation)


차가 없어요

(tap for translation)

You’ll notice a couple of things here, being the astute learner that you are. The first is that 이 is used if the word has a 받침. If the word ends in a vowel then you use 가.

매튜 선생
This consonant-vowel agreement rule was tricky for me when I learned Korean because I understood in my head that particles usually have to follow the consonant-vowel pattern, but it can be harder to make your tongue do the right thing. Sometimes you will catch yourself saying “물가 없어요,” and that’s okay. Koreans are likely to brush it off with a smile and compliment you on how smart you are to be able to learn Korean.

Another thing you’ll notice is the continuity of the sounds here. I think that because Korean has particles, and the particles match with the flow of the words, it can be very hard to distinguish where one word stops and another word begins. Korean is really going to mess with your ears. We’ve mentioned that developing the ability to listen to Korean is a crucial skill to have. Upon hearing Korean for the first time many westerners comment that it doesn’t sound like a language, but just sounds like a bunch of mumbled nonsense. Keep listening carefully and copying the pronunciation of our esteemed native speakers. If you do that you’ll quickly develop an ear (and tongue) for the language.

In the case of “물이 있어요" the sound of the 이 subject particle is indistinguishable from the first sound in 있어요. That means that it almost sounds the same whether or not we have a subject marker at all (as in “물 있어요"). These kinds of situations contribute to the Korean penchant for dropping things out of sentences. Originally the subject of the sentence should always be marked, however you’ll often hear Koreans say, “물 있어요" or “물 없어요". Indeed, in short and simple statements like this the intended meaning is abundantly clear with or without grammatical markers.

Just one moreFragment quick note on pronunciation. You can see our carry-over pronunciation rules in effect here. 있어요 is going to read like 이써요, and moreFragment notably 없어요 is going to read like 업서요. You’ll recall that in cases like this where we have a double-받침 the second letter carries over onto the next block and the first one remains on the bottom of the first block.
