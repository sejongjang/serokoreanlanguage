Demonstratives

Chapter Insight

Hurray, you made it to the last grammar chapter! This chapter on demonstratives is one of the most interesting chapters in this program. Sometimes languages seem to develop chaotically, but in this case, Korean is no doubt one of the most sensical and logical languages in existence.

What is a demonstrative and why is it important enough to be talked about in an introductory-level Korean course? Well for your information we’ve already used three demonstratives just in the introductory paragraph. Demonstratives are words we use to point to things around us, and in Korean they are closely related to many common pronouns as well. Words like, “this”, “that”, “here”, “there”, “this kind”, “that way”, “this much” etc. are all demonstratives.

So what’s so fancy about Korean demonstratives? Well they follow a near-perfect pattern, which can be called the 이/그/저/어 pattern. This pattern is based off of the relative location between the speaker, listener, and objects in question. Relative relationships are something you already understand naturally as an English speaker. For example, you innately understand the difference between “this chair” and “that chair”, right? “This chair” is close to the speaker and not necessarily close the listener. “That chair” is not close to the speaker and may or may not be close to the listener.

Korean functions in a similar way, however you’ll find that with Korean you have more fine-tuned controls. In English we have “this”, and “that”. Korean has “this”, “that”, and “that thing close to neither of us”. Let's take a look at our first Korean demonstratives now.

Word
Meaning

this

그

that

저

어느

Here are our first demonstratives, but take careful note that these are adjectives. English is a little bit weird because the words this/that can function both as an adjective and a pronoun. In Korean the pronoun version of 이/그/저 is 이것/그것/저것 (lit. "this/that" + "thing"). Let's talk about each of these words one at a time.

“그” is the next word, meaning “that”, and the mechanics of it function just like “이”. The important thing to remember here is that what you are signaling with “그” is something that is closer to the listener, not the speaker. 그 is also used as the word for abstract things that don’t have a measurable distance associated with them. For example:

Since "that class" and "that game" don't have a measureable distance from the speaker or listener we can use "그" to refer to them generally. Next up we have the word “저”. 저 is used to point to things that are close to neither the speaker, nor the listener. When describing something far away this is the word to use. In English we use "that" for both 그 and 저.

At this point it should be mentioned, the fact that in English when something is plural the demonstrative usually changes. For example, “this chair” becomes “these chairs”. Korean has none of this nonsense and you can use the same demonstratives regardless. To make it plural just add “들” onto the noun.

Meaning

Before moving on we'll just tell you that while we said “이”, and “그” are strictly adjectives, there is one sort-of exception. “이 사람(들)” and “그 사람(들)” can be abbreviated to “이(들)” and “그(들)”. 이 and 그 are male pronouns when used in singular form, but may apply to men and women when plural (sort of like the English pronoun “you guys”).
