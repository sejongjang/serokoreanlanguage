Chapter 23

Possession and Association

Chapter Insight

In this chapter we’ll be exploring the uses of a very important new particle—the possessive marker. While possessives in English can be confusing it’s very simple in Korean.

Jae-young’s bike, Seung-woo’s boat, the flag of South Korea, the first day of school—all of these demonstrate possession. The bike is of Jae-young, the flag is of Korea. In communication it is useful to categorize things in these ways. It’s an important way to specify the relationships things in the world have.

In Korean, ownership and categorization are both expressed through a single, powerful particle—의. This particle, like all other Korean particles, sticks onto the end of nouns, and defines that noun’s function. We can basically think of 의 as the Korean version of “Apostrophe-S”. This is because 의 follows the same order. 재영의 자전거 = Jae-young’s bike. Fortunately 의 doesn’t have any alternate form and stays the same whether it comes after a consonant or a vowel. For example 우리의/한국의.

However, an important thing to note is 의's pronunciation. The possesive 의 found at the end of a noun is pronounced as 에. This is the result of a chagne over time in korean speakers electing to differentiate it with words that actually contain 의 in the world, usually meaning intention or righteousness, and to make speaking flow better as 에 at the end of nouns flows a lot better than 의 does pronunciation wise. But since 에 already has it's grammatical role then we can see why they still use 의 instead of just changing it completely to 에. Therefore, 우리의 = 우리에 and 한국의 = 한국에(한구게) pronunciation wise.

The problem is that you don’t always pronounce it as 에. When 의 appears combined with other letters, or inside of words it’s more often pronounced with it’s original 으-이, sound. You can hear this in examples like 거의 (Almost), 의견 (Opinion), and 띄다 (To stand out).
