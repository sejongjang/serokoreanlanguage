One last thing to cover now. A final important usage of the native-Korean numbering system is in telling hours. In order to do this we combine our cardinal numbers with the hour marker “시”. So 한시 is 1 o'clock, 두시 is 2 o’clock, 세시 is 3 o’clock, and so on. In what amounts to lingual Frankenstein however, all other units of time are based in Chinese numbers, including years, months, minutes, and seconds. I don’t know why this is, it’s just the way it is. The system for telling time is perhaps the freakiest mix up of Chinese and Korean systems you’ll find, but you’ll quickly get used to it considering how often you use it.

Another thing to be aware of is that you can also add “반” onto the hour to make it “half past”. 반 actually means “half” so it makes sense. 네시반 (usually written as 4시반) is 4:30.

Finally to ask what time it is you can use the question word 몇 (remember ㅊ is a T-stop consonant so this sounds like 몉). 몇 means “how many” and you can stick it in front of 시 to produce 몇시. With our copula the full sentence becomes “몇시예요?”—“What time is it?”. It’s not how English speakers would think to ask, but it makes sense if you think about it—essentially “How many hours have passed (in the day)”. We can also combine 몇 with the other counters like 개 to ask “몃개예요?”—“How many is it?”. Naturally “몇개 있어요?”—“How many are there”—also works. Let's look at some examples.
