이/가 is an important particle that will allow us to use the existence verbs 있다 and 없다. Remember our little consonant-vowel agreement rule? Well it turns out that grammatical markers (particles) in Korean typically follow this rule. 이/가 marks the subject of the sentence (amazing right). That means we can use this particle to tell us whether we have dogs chasing cats or cats chasing dogs. Very useful.

Let's see what this looks like with our existence verbs.

물이 있어요

(tap for translation)


물이 없어요

(tap for translation)


차가 있어요

(tap for translation)


차가 없어요

(tap for translation)

You’ll notice a couple of things here, being the astute learner that you are. The first is that 이 is used if the word has a 받침. If the word ends in a vowel then you use 가.

매튜 선생
This consonant-vowel agreement rule was tricky for me when I learned Korean because I understood in my head that particles usually have to follow the consonant-vowel pattern, but it can be harder to make your tongue do the right thing. Sometimes you will catch yourself saying “물가 없어요,” and that’s okay. Koreans are likely to brush it off with a smile and compliment you on how smart you are to be able to learn Korean.

Historically Korea has received relatively minimal attention internationally, however since the invention of Hangul it’s gained tremendous prestige and Korea is playing an ever more important role politically, economically, and especially culturally in the modern world. More and more people are learning about Korea and how’s it’s unique from its bigger and traditionally more influential neighbors, China and Japan.

That’s not to say you shouldn’t experiment making new sentences with the words we teach you. Just consider this as a forewarning that Korean words are not exact equivalents of what they translate to in English. That’s why translation is such messy business, and why it’s always better to understand things in the original language.
