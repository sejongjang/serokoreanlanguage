The second main negator verb form we will learn is ~지 못하다. This one means “cannot” and is added to verbs just the same as ~지 않다. Examples of ~지 못하다 can be seen in the following examples

가지 못했어요
가지 못해요
가지 못할거예요
먹지 못했어요
먹지 못해요
먹지 못할거예요

However, just like with 않다 there are shorter ways to use 못하다. One is used with nouns and the other is attached to verbs just like ~지 못하다.

The negation verb we use with nouns is “못하다". This can be used by itself or put in front of nouns like 공부(study) and would be seen used like, “공부를 못했어요" meaning “I couldn’t study”. Try using 못하다 with the following nouns and make some sentences in past, present, and future tenses!

숙제(homework),
운전(drive),
요리(cooking),
등산(hiking),
야구(baseball)

Now what do we use with verbs? This is also so simple that most Koreans also use this form in everyday conversations. You just add 못 to whatever verb you want and it will negate it. Let’s look at some examples.

못가요 (I can’t go),
못먹었어요(I couldn’t eat),
못잘거예요(I won’t be able to sleep),
못봐요(I can’t see/watch).

There is an important difference to recognize between ~지 않다(안하다) and ~지 못하다. When we use 않다 we are saying that we choose to not do something or that something did not happen. On the other hand, 못하다 is used to express something that cannot be done even if you wanted to. For example, to say “I didn’t do my homework” we would say “숙제를 안했어요" denoting that we could have done our homework but chose not to. If we say, “숙제를 못했어요" then that means we wanted to do our homework but something prevented us from doing it.

Bonus: Koreans sometimes employ double negatives in ways similar to English (it’s also fun like English). For example, Jessica: “She didn’t say she’s leaving.” Jaeyoung: “Well she didn’t say she’s not leaving.”—can be translated as 제시카: “그 사람은 떠난다는 말이 없어요.” 재영: “그러긴 한데 떠나지 않겠다는 말도 없잖아요”. This is a case where the rules of English grammar don’t allow it, but subject is normally assumed from context in Korean.
