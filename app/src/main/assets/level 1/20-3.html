Why hasn’t North Korea been democratized yet?

Since its inception, North Korea’s communist government has proven to be one of the most resilient and determined dynastic-regimes in modern history, outlasting even the Soviet Union. Beginning after the end of the Korean war, the DPRK has dedicated a huge amount of its resources to military ventures founded upon the false narrative that North Korea is the only free sanctuary left in the world. The narrative taught to every North Korean citizen from the time they are young is that the United Nations (western civilization) and specifically the United States committed inumerable rapes, murders, and a variety of inhumane actions such as impaling children on bayonets, severing people’s limbs off, and burning Koreans alive during the Korean War. Despite having no proof of these heinous acts, the DPRK government built the Shinchon American Atrocities Museum to showcase paintings and models of the indictments against the US and encourage hatred toward any western influence.

However, the falsities don’t stop there unfortunately. Schools teach that North Korea is abundantly prosperous in comparison to their neighbors to the south. According to North Korean beliefs, due to the evils of capitalism and liberalism, South Korea and many other countries throughout the world beg for food, live in huts, and suffer from disease and malnutrition. To them, the only protection from the evils and hardships that capitalism inevitably brings with it is the Korean version of socialism known as, Juche (주체 사상). Despite everyone in the free world knowing that South Korea is one of the most prosperous and advanced nations in the world, North Koreans would never know this because travel outside of North Korea being strictly forbidden. Actually, even travel between provinces within the DPRK is heavily restricted to those who have government approved passes. With that in mind, we can now see that fantasies like that of George Orwell’s book 1984 are no longer fiction. Because the citizenry actually believes what the government tells them, they have no reason to fight against the government for it acts as the only ‘protection’ from the evils of the outside world. As a whole, the country looks to the great leader Kim Jung-eun (김정은) and the government for leadership and strength, so any democratization movements have been small and, due to communication restrictions, have never reached the entirety of the country. Injustices committed against citizens are well known throughout the north but trying to organize an overthrow of the government is an uphill battle. The DPRK government consistently taps phones, spies on suspicious activity amongst the lower classes or ‘unloyal classes’, restricts inter-provincial communication and travel, all while exercising extreme authoritarian control over almost every public service.

The short answer is there are 2 distinct reasons why after over 70 years of suffering under communist dictatorship North Korea has not been democratized yet. The first being the trust the public has in the government to protect them from the made up dangers of the outside world. Thus, despite any injustices the government may do to the people, it would hypothetically be better than if another western country took over and enslaved everyone, ironic right? The second is because of the extent of control the government has over the lives of the populous. Without access to weapons, ways to coordinate country-wide protests, ability to freely travel within the country, and influence in the legal system there have been no effective or successful reform movements to this day.
