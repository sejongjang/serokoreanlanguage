Before we get into what is located in korean homes an essential skill to have is understanding how to get there in the first place! Finding address' in our home countries are already hard enough let alone trying to do the same in foreign ones. Korea's zoning and adress systems differ very widely for the most part with many western countries, states, cities, and towns.

In order to find an address in Korea without the help of the internet we need to understand how Korea's provinces and cities are organized. The first the thing to know is that Korea has 8 provinces and 9 administrative districts around highly populated cities or autonomus administrative regions that all have representatives in Korea's congress or parliament.

This is quite confusing but let's learn some vocab so we can start figuring out how everything is organized what it's called.

제가 커피예요.

I am coffee(?!?!)

(tap for translation)


저는 커피요.

As for me it('ll) be coffee.

(tap for translation)

Now you're probably wondering if there's a mispelling here but no, there isn't. When responding with your preference about something 이에요/예요 is abbreviated to 요. Also remember that when 저 is used with the subject marker (저+가) it becomes “제”. You can see above that when 이/가 is used with the copula(이다) it marks direct equivallency to the subject, but when we use 은/는 something interesting happens. Suddenly we can use the verb "to be" in more loose and interesting ways.

제영씨는 홍차예요 녹차예요?

What about you Jaeyoung, black tea or green tea?

(tap for translation)


녹차요. 감사합니다.

Green tea. Thank you.

(tap for translation)

There’s a few pearls of knowledge to glean from this example. In this case, 은/는 is rendered as “what about”. This has a very similar meaning to “as for”. Without knowing much about the context, it’s clear that Ashley is changing the topic of the conversation to Jaeyoung. (specifically Jaeyoung’s preference in beverage). Perhaps Ashley is going to the cafe on luch break and is asking other people in the office if they'd like anything.

You’ll also notice that in the above example there is no word for “or” in the Korean sentence. While there are a couple of words that can be used similarly to the English word “or”, it’s also common to merely list two alternatives in a row, in a questioning tone of voice, as shown in the example sentence above. (Bonus, yay! Now you know one way to say “or” in Korean.)
