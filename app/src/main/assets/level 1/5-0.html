Chapter 5

Stating What Something Is

Chapter Insight

Alright, are you ready for your first chapter on Korean grammar? Don’t sweat, this won’t be too tricky. We’re going to be talking about the copula. “Wait... The co-pu-la-what-ah?”

The copula is the most important verb in any language. It’s the verb “to be”. This is the verb that allows you to say A = B. A few examples will no doubt suffice. In English the verb to be is “is”, “am”, or “are”. For example: “This is a white chair.”, “I am exhausted.”, and “You are a fool.” (just kidding I know you’re super smart). In all of these cases A = B, or in other words the thing presented before the verb is characterized by the thing after the verb. In English the copula is also known as a “Linking Verb” because it links two things together.

In Korean the copula is especially important because Korean is a verb-centric language. Other parts of the sentence can often be omitted, but you must always have a verb in proper Korean sentences. Now is also a good time to make an important distinction between English and Korean grammar. In English we follow a strict Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) sentence structure. In the sentence, " I eat apples", "I" is the subject, "eat" is the verb, and "apples" are the object being acted upon by the subject.

Now get ready to have your mind blown, because Korean follows a different pattern. In Korean the word order is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV). Following this pattern, our previously stated sentence would now be organized as, "I apples eat". A little strange right? I mentioned earlier that Korean sentences always need a verb. According to this new information that crucially important verb must always come at the end of the sentence. You don't get too many always statements in language, but always having a verb and always putting it at the end are two rules you can always stick to.

If we impose this Korean order onto our math equation it's a little bit awkward… Okay really awkward. We have A, B, = . While it may seem counterintuitive, that's exactly how Korean works. This SOV order is consistent whether it's a linking verb or any other. In fact, Korean is much more consistent in this regard than English is. In English you change the word order when you ask questions (“Is that your house”). So, in English the verb sometimes comes first. Thankfully Korean has none of this confusing silliness and when you ask questions you just raise the pitch in your voice at the end of the sentence.
