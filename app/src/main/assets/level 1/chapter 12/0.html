So what do you think? It doesn't really resemble the other numbers (하나, 둘, 셋, 넷, 다섯, 여섯, 일곱, 여덟, 아홉, 열) does it? But not so bad right? One thing you’ll notice about Chinese-based vocabulary is that the spelling is more simple. In fact, you will never find double-받침 present in Chinese-based vocabulary. Clearly those who converted the Chinese characters into 한글 made efforts to ensure simplification. That helps because once you recognize spelling conventions you can more easily determine word origin. That will in turn help you know how to deal with the words, as we’ll see later on. Native Korean numbers may have evolved in a way a little bit more like the western ones, however the Chinese have a very different take on the numbering system. Let me explain.

Have you ever thought about how arbitrary the names we give to numbers are? In English we go from one, ten, hundred, and thousand, to ten thousand. So we make a new word the first three times we add a zero, but then suddenly we combine two existing words to create a new unit, "ten-thousand"? Why do we do this? Well the answer is, of course, to make things easier. Just imagine if we had to remember a new word every time we added a zero. It would probably be quite overwhelming.

코디 선생
Learning Korean at first I saw no reason to have two number systems and was very confused in which situations I should use the Korean or Chinese number systems. I realize now you can compare Chinese and Korean to Latin/Greek and English. Our version of the Latin numbers are seen in examples like mono-theism (belief in ONE god), bi-cycle (TWO wheels), tri-dent (THREE-pronged-spear), etc. So now the concept of having two number systems in Korean doesn't seem that weird anymore! If you review chapter 9, you will see that the korean numbers are usually only used for counting things and people up until ~49, age, and orders (first, second, third, etc.). Things like calendar dates, numbers over 49, math, codes, phone numbers and the like will all be in the chinese numbers. So dont worry too much at first, its okay to mess up! When in doubt, just trying using the Chinese. So try to get this system down extra well.

The break with Chinese-based numbering systems isn’t that they make a new unit every time they add a zero—they merely start the new unit creation one zero later. In Chinese numbers you go from one (일), to ten (십), to hundred (백), to thousand (천), to another new unit “만”—ten thousand. This means that to say 100,000 you don’t say “one-hundred thousand”, rather you would say “ten ten-thousands”.
