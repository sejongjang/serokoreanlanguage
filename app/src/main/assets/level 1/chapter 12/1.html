Confused yet? Just wait till we get into bigger numbers. In English, following the thousands unit we make a new unit for every three zeros added. So, we have 1000 (thousand), 1,000,000 (million), 1,000,000,000 (billion), and so on. Since Chinese-based numbers have another higher base unit than 1,000 they create a new unit for every 4 zeros added. The result is 만 (ten thousand), 억 (one-hundred million), and 조 (1 trillion). It does seem like they are skipping a lot of numbers in there, but the nice thing is that you actually have fewer units to remember. Here’s a full chart to help you settle this in your brain now.

You can see that both Chinese and Latin-based systems meet up at one trillion. Since we make new units for every 3 zeroes and they make new units for every 4 zeros, it makes sense that the unit naming scheme would meet up when we have 12 zeros. In case you math nerds are interested the two systems meet up again at one septillion which is 10^24 (1 with 24 zeros behind it).

Unless you’re a computer scientist you're not likely to ever hear about such absurdly large numbers. However you should be aware that the Korean currency, the Won₩ (원), is usually valued at somewhere around 1000-1200 Won per one US Dollar. That doesn’t mean that their currency is worthless, but that they just deal with much higher numbers with their money. We have a one dollar (or pound or rand) bill and they have a 천원(1000 Won) bill. Also, according to averagesalarysurvey.com, the average gross salary in South Korea is 63,898,594 Won. That equates to about $55,851. Anyone would dream of making ~64 million dollars a year, but in South Korea 64 million won is only just average.

You can imagine how big these numbers get when you deal with things like government budgets or business investments, so the unit for one trillion, “조”, is actually used quite commonly. Here again it’s just a matter of training your brain to be able to work with the new system. It’s not any better or worse than the western system, just different.

To say what month of the year it is in Korean they don’t have strange unique words for every month, but rather they have each month assigned to a number. Starting at January the months go in order from one to twelve, just like we would write them when writing the date.

You are observant enough to see that 유월(June) and 시월(October) seem to have lost their 받침. This mutation occurred naturally over time because again, 유월 and 시월 are easier to say than 육월 and 십월. Still these minor variations should be easier to remember than if they had a unique word for every month, right? (That’s what Koreans go through when they learn English). The “월” part of these words does mean month, and is logically based off of the Chinese character 月, for “moon”.

What about years? Well those are even easier. The word for year is “년" and we can combine it with any year in history, say 1957년, to specify the date. 기원전 and 서기 are used for B.C. and A.D. respectively. So 188년 서기.

For days we use the word “일" which comes from 日, the Chinese character for “Sun”. You can say 7월 9일 for July 9th or 2월 23일 for February 23rd. With the words 년 and 일 our Chinese-based numbers don’t change
