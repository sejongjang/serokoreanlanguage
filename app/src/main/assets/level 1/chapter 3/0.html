Chapter 3

Double Consonants and Special Cases

While 한글 is highly logical and extremely easy to learn there are a few more mysterious behaviors and letters we need to talk about in this short chapter before moving on. After finishing this chapter you'll be able to read all but the most obscure or rare 한글 perfectly (the kind of stuff natives don't know how to read for sure either).

야

If we already had compound-badchim, what the heck is a double consonant? used all last chapter. This similarity is completely intentional and logical. In Korean taking a regular vowel and adding another line onto it adds a “Y” sound to the beginning of the vowel. So in this case taking the vowel 아 and adding a line made it into a “yah” as in “yacht” sound. You’ll find this to be a consistent rule of Hangul.

What happens if we put some of our consonants, like ㄱ or ㄴ, together with ㅑ?

까

These sorts of combinations sound positively foreign to English speakers but are completely normal in Korean. You’ll often see consonants followed immediately by a “Y’ sound. Granted there are a few cases in English where something similar occurs. When you say the word “new” you’re actually saying something close to “nyu”.

Based on what I just told you, you’re no doubt wondering “What about 갹, 갼, 냑, and 냔?” Although these are technically valid combinations, they are not commonly used blocks. In fact, out of the over 3,000 possible block combinations in Korean, a mere 500 combinations constitute 99% the language. The other couple thousand are used only in extremely rare and/or ancient Korean. There are also some combinations that have simply never been used. This no doubt has something to do with Hangul being created long after the spoken-language was already well established. King Sejong actually made a system that was more powerful and flexible than the language itself needed. Pretty impressive.

This practice below should be a no-brainer:

아야

마냐

갸아

얍

란야

샷

냡

랴

닷야

We should note that there are some consonant combinations that never combine with 야. For example, you will never see ㄴ/ㄹ and ㄹ/ㄹ carry-over sound combined with 야 (like 달랴 or 난랴—seriously these are weird).

Also, recall that we taught you ㅅ makes an "s" or "sh" sound. As you saw above, ㅅ combined with 야 gives us a "sha" sound. This rules is true across the board. ㅅ will make an "sh" sound when combined with any y-vowel. In the next pages we'll be combining the double-line y-vowels like 야 with their regular counterparts, since just adding a "Y" sound is a piece of cake.
