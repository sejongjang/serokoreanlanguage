After dinner you might watch 티뷔 or play games until bedtime. If your friend has a spare room, you’ll likely be ushered in there. Again, depending on the wealth/lifestyle of your friend they may have a spare 침대 (bed) in the 침대실 (bedroom), or you may be sleeping on the floor. Sleeping on the floor is another Korean tradition. In this case your friend will get you a 매트 (mat) and 이불 (thick blankets for sleeping). 매트 is a fairly new concept. In older times Koreans slept with very little padding underneath them. 이불 is designed to be able to fold up and be put away during the daytime when you aren’t using it. There are still many Koreans who sleep on the floor and not in a bed.

If you and your friend are of the same gender and they live in a smaller home, they may also invite you to sleep in their bed with you. Don’t take this the wrong way as Koreans are still quite conservative when it comes to sexual relations. Sharing a sleeping space is the same as sharing food and is a natural sign of friendship. Koreans would use another word—정. The word 정 refers to something more than friendship. It implies trust and a deep connection. We’ll be talking more about 정 in future cultural discussions.

Once you are settled down you can say goodnight to your friend “잘 자요”, and turn off the 불 (lights/fire). If it’s too hot you can cool of the room using the 에어컨 (air conditioner). Usually there is one air conditioning nit in each bedroom in Korea. If it’s cold you can ask your friend to adjust the 난방 (centralized heating). Finally you can rest your head on your 베개 (pillow).

If you wake up in time in the morning you can help your friend make breakfast in the 부엌 (kitchen). You can get 김치 (kimchi) and side dishes out of the 냉장고 (refrigerator). Your friend may also ask you to get something out of the 냉동실 (freezer). You can also scoop rice out of the 밥솥 (rice cooker) and into a 그릇 (bowl). You will find 접시 (plates) in the 찬장 (cupboard).
