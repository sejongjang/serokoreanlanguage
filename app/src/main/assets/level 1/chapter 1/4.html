다

ㄷ is our next letter, which is pronounced “D” (as in down). As you can tell by its shape, ㄷ is closely related to it’s brother ㄴ. It is also pronounced with more of a flicking sound than a heavy sound. Also like ㄴ, ㄷ can sound like a different letter under certain circumstances. In this case ㄷ can sometimes make a “T” sound at the beginning of words. Of course, sounds are relative, and this is just categorizing it as English speakers hear it. As you make the “N”, “D”, and “T” sounds, you'll realize that they all involve flicking the front of the tongue off the top of the mouth just back of your front teeth. Again, similar shapes make similar sounds as shown on the following expanded ㄴ pronunciation chart.

ㄷ is also the first T-stop badchim letter, meaning that when ㄷ appears on the bottom of a syllable block it makes a T-stop sound similar to what we find in English words like “got”. Unlike English, Korean does not make sounds that aren’t written in the spelling. Try saying the English word “dud” once. The truth is that you probably made not one, but two vowel sounds in this word. You probably said, “duh-deu”. In Korean they never say vowels that aren’t written in. If we listen to 갇, or 닫 it just cuts off with the D-sound, unlike the English word “dud”. Give it a listen then practice it for yourself. There’s a few more letters coming up that also make a T-stop sound in the badchim position.
