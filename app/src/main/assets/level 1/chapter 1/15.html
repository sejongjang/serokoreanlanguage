We've done it! We've reached our last regular consonant, ㅎ! Hurray, aren’t you happy? Can you guess what the last letter is?

하

That’s right, ㅎ corresponds to the English “H” sound, as found in words like “heat”, “harsh”, and “hoot”. In this unique case, ㅎ's non-aspirated partner ㅇ does not make a similar sound. That’s not to say that there’s no relation between the two, however. The shape of the mouth is actually very similar when ㅎ and ㅇ appear at the beginning of a syllable block. The difference is that you’re blowing air out of your mouth with ㅎ (aspirated). Again with ㅎ there’s no other letter sound to compare it to than H, so sorry—you’re out of luck if you wanted a chart.

ㅎ is also a bit of an exception to the add-one-line-to-make-aspirated rule. In this case you add two lines. If you have a hard time remembering it, just remember ㅎ is ㅇ’s aspirated partner that likes to wear a hat.

I know you wanted to end this chapter easily, but ㅎ has some mysterious powers and behaviors we need to talk about. The first thing to notice is the carry-over sound ㅎ makes in words like 닳아. The rule here is that when ㅎ comes before a ㅇ it becomes silent like ㅇ. If it come after ㅇ it isn't silenced because ㅇ's "ng" pronunciation doesn't silence the ㅎ. Instead we will have it's regular "H" pronunciation. Also, since we brought up 닳아, you can see that ㅀ is our final compound badchim. This one is fairly well used.

The next thing to note is that ㅎ has an unusual effect on nearby non-aspirated consonants. Listen to 갛게 and 갖하 again. They are actually pronounced as 가케 and 가차! When ㅎ meets a non-aspirated letter that has an aspirated counterpart, it magically transforms it into the aspirated version! Mindblowing right?

Finally, when ㅎ is in the badchim position and it can't carry-over it is silent (as you saw above with "맣"). This bevahior is just like what happens when H comes at the end of an English word, for example "messiah".
