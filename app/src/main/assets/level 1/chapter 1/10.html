Great work so far we're more than halfway done with the main consonants now. Next up to bat we’ve got the Korean version of the “J” sound.

자

ㅈ follows the pattern of being more light and poppy than the heavy English “J” sound we're used to in words like, “judge”, “John”, “jade”, etc. Think instead of the “J” sound you make in words like “gene”, “jitter”, “Jeff”. This sound is going to border very closely to the English “Ch” sound, and in many cases if a word begins with a ㅈ, English speakers will actually here a “Ch” sound. The important thing to understand here is that ㅈ, and all the consonants we've learned up to this point (ㄱ, ㄴ, ㄷ, ㄹ, ㅁ, ㅂ, ㅅ, and ㅇ) have been non-aspirated sounds.

What is aspiration? Let's try an experiment. Hold your hand up in front of you mouth and say these words: "pop", "case", and "tell". You felt it right? The first letter of each of these words is aspirated, meaning you release a puff of air when you say it. Now hold your hand up again and let's try a few more words: "bag", "gone", and "night". How about that, no puff of air! That's because these are non-aspirated consonant sounds. And just like "bag", "gone", and "night", when you say any of the letters we've learned so far(ㄱ, ㄴ, ㄷ, ㄹ, ㅁ, ㅂ, ㅅ, ㅇ and ㅈ) there should be no puff of air.
