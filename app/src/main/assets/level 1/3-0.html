While 한글 is highly logical and extremely easy to learn there are a few more mysterious behaviors and letters we need to talk about here shortly before moving on. After finishing this chapter you'll be able to read all but the most obscure or rare 한글 (the kind of stuff natives don't know how to read for sure either).

In Chapter 1 we talked about regular consonants and aspirated consonants. There are five regular consonants (ㄱ, ㄷ, ㅂ, ㅇ, and ㅈ) which have aspirated versions (ㅊ, ㅋ, ㅌ, ㅍ, and ㅎ). You remember that aspirated letters have another line added, but it turns out that four of these letters (ㄱ, ㄷ, ㅂ, and ㅈ) as well as another consonant (ㅅ) all have a "tensed" version. These tensed-cosonants are made by doubling the letter. The first one we'll look at is ㄲ.

까

These tensed-consonants are a little bit stronger than the regular ones. Give it a listen a listen again and compare it with 가.

가

까

You can feel it right? There’s something to these tensed consonants that isn’t there in non-tensed ones. There are two problems you’ll encounter here, however. First, on the listening front you may be able to hear the difference when it’s clearly enunciated for you like this, but Koreans talk fast. You have to be able to distinguish tensation in regular conversation, which is much more difficult.

Secondly, when it comes to pronouncing these tensed consonants, foreigners tend to just say the original consonant but louder. Please take this to heart now, because it can save you from embarrassment later. Tensed consonants are not pronounced by saying them louder than their regular counterparts. But if they’re not louder, what’s different about them?

Essentially there are two distinguishing factors between regular consonants and tensed ones. The first thing is that tensed consonants start a little higher than normal. While Korean gratefully has no system of tones like Thai or Chinese do, one thing about these double consonants is that they do have a sort of falling tone to them. Again, listen carefully and try to replicate.

When I got to Korea and began speaking with many people I was able to start hearing the difference between regular and tensed-consonants fairly quickly, but it took a couple of years and tons of practice to figure out exactly how to pronounce these tensed consonants in a way that made it so Koreans knew which letter I was saying. It turns out that if I had just known about these two fairly easy guidelines I would have been able to ensure that Koreans knew whether I mean 굴 or 꿀 from the beginning. You, lucky reader, get this information without having to figure it out for yourself the hard way.

The other thing about tensed consonants is that they have a different timing to them than regular letters do. It’s almost as if you take an ever so tiny pause right before you say the letter. This adds tension. It’s like pulling back just a little bit harder on a rubber band before launching it at your unsuspecting target. Since this tension builds up a little bit longer, the release is a little bit more dramatic. Again, this doesn’t really mean louder, just different.
