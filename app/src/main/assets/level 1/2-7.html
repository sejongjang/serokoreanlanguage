Alright! We made it to our last vowel, 에/예.

에

예

에 is a vowel which, in the original version of 한글 (in the times of King Sejong), had a sound distinct and different from 애. Over time, however, they’ve become very similar. Some might even say indistinguishable. All languages undergo this drifting process in which sounds, letters, words, and accents—everything—is subject to change over time. This modern 에 sound is the pure “eh” you’re familiar with in words like "met", “empty”, and “elevator”. The fundamental difference between 애 and 에 is highlighted perfectly by the debate raging in the English speaking world over how to pronounce the word “egg”. You have the group that insists it ought to be pronounced with more of an “A” sound, and the group that argues that egg is spelled with an E and should thus be pronounced with a pure “eh” sound.

In truth there's very little difference between the two and you likely wouldn't even notice whether your friend says Ae-gg or Eh-gg when they're talking fast. In Korea they've already gotten over the fact that this difference is not important. As a result of this, it doesn't really matter what you say when you see 애 or 에. If you are a pronunciation purist however, 애 correlates with the A sound (like “Ae-gg”) and 에 is more the pure "E" sound (like “Ehh-gg”).

The one compound-vowel for 에 is 웨.

웨

Now we have yet another vowel sound that sounds the same as "외" and "왜". *Sigh*. There's really no way to tell them apart just from listening. Practice with reading and writing will help you know when to use each vowel.
