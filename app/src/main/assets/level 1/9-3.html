In language cardinal numbers are also very important. Cardinal numbers tell us how much of something there is. In English cardinal numbers don’t change from their original form whether it’s five eggs, or twenty umbrellas. Unfortunately, there is a little change in Korean. This change has to do with counting words or “counters”.

Counters are present to some extent in every language. In English we say “three sheets of paper” as opposed to “three papers” (which could mean a number of different things). A counter tells us to pay attention to the quantity of something rather than other features about it. East Asian languages use counters frequently.

In English the word “sheet” refers exclusively to things that are thin and flat. However, a feature of English is that you can often use what you are talking about as the counter. So if we have twelve hamburgers, we don’t need a separate counter word because a hamburger is an unmistakable unit. Metal on the other hand is ambiguous and should have a counter attached as in “eight sheets of steel” or “two bars of iron”.

While English is helpful in that it often doesn't require you to learn separate counter words, Korean is nice because it is consistent. Counting anything in Korean requires an associated counter word. Now before you bemoan the presence of such an onerous grammatical requirement I should tell you that there’s an easy shortcut to deal with this.

개 is a counter word that means “thing”. This is the most common counter word you’ll encounter (hehe), and is extremely flexible. In fact, if you were wanting to say you saw “three cars” and you forgot the counter for large electronic devices or motorized machines (대), you could simply say “차 세개". We only have to worry about the first four numbers 1-4, as all other numbers stay in their original form when counters are attached to them.

Meaning

한개

one (thing)

두개

two (things)

With all other number 개 sticks directly onto the end without the base number changing (ex. 열여섯개, 스물아홉개, 마흔개). You can also see that 개 sticks directly onto the end of the number. This is another place in English where we normally have spaces but Korean doesn’t. Now we'll show you just a few of the most common counters.
